Part 1: Selfie - The Wrong Way vs. The Right Way

Understanding how distance and focal length affect portrait photography. Compare a close-up selfie with a zoomed-in photo taken from further away.

Close-up Selfie
Taken from close distance - notice the distortion

Why does this happen?

The difference in these images demonstrates the relationship between camera distance and perspective distortion. When photographing from close up, features closer to the camera (like the nose) appear disproportionately large compared to features further away (like the ears). This creates the unflattering "selfie distortion" effect.

By stepping back and using optical zoom, we maintain the same framing while reducing perspective distortion. The longer focal length compresses the depth, making facial features appear more proportional and natural - this is why professional portrait photographers typically use longer lenses and shoot from greater distances.

Part 2: Architectural Perspective Compression

Exploring how focal length affects the perception of depth and distance in architectural photography.

Wide Angle (Close Position)

Telephoto (Distant Position)

The Compression Effect

This demonstrates perspective compression in architectural photography. The wide-angle shot (left) taken from closer to the subject emphasizes the depth and distance between foreground and background elements, making the scene appear more three-dimensional and spacious.

The telephoto shot (right) taken from further away with zoom creates a "flattened" or compressed appearance. Background elements appear closer to foreground elements, and the sense of depth is reduced. This compression effect is commonly used in cinematography to create different emotional impacts and visual storytelling effects.

Part 3: The Dolly Zoom Effect

The famous "Vertigo Shot" - simultaneously moving the camera back while zooming in to create a disorienting effect where the subject stays the same size but the background changes dramatically.

Dolly Zoom Animation
Camera moves back while zooming in - notice how the background changes while the subject remains constant

The Hitchcock Effect

The dolly zoom, also known as the "Vertigo shot" after its famous use in Alfred Hitchcock's film, creates a surreal visual effect. By simultaneously moving the camera away from the subject while zooming in (or vice versa), the main subject maintains its size in the frame while the background dramatically changes perspective.

This technique exploits the relationship between focal length and perspective. As we zoom in, we're increasing the focal length, which compresses the background. As we move away, we're changing our physical relationship to the scene. The combination creates an unsettling effect where the spatial relationships in the image seem to shift in impossible ways.

Note: This effect works because focal length affects perspective compression, while physical camera position affects the relative size of objects. By carefully balancing these two factors, we can keep one element constant while dramatically altering others.
